Essay — AI & Society
May 2026
Response & Reflection

What Kind of World Are We Building?

Thinking Machines Lab released something technically remarkable this month. But the harder question — one the whole industry is avoiding — is what we are trading away in the race to build it.

Topics: AI, Labour, Democracy, Warm Data, Human Futures
A note to Mira and the team at Thinking Machines Lab

What you built this month is not incremental. Breaking the turn-based architecture — giving AI the ability to think alongside a person rather than wait for them to finish — opens domains that have been structurally closed since this field began. The doctor mid-examination. The teacher present in the exact moment a student’s understanding shifts. The researcher thinking out loud. These are the people who could never fit their work into a prompt. You reached them. That matters, and this essay begins by saying so clearly.

What follows is not a critique of the model. It is an attempt to map the world the model is being released into — the historical patterns that tend to determine who benefits from breakthroughs like yours, the structural forces that run against the warm intentions behind the technology, and the paths that exist toward something better. It is also an acknowledgment that those structural forces are larger than any lab can redirect alone.

But you are not just any lab right now. You have built something that works, and that gives you a credibility and a platform that most people in this conversation do not have. What we hope this encourages you to do is use it — to say from inside the room what the structural conditions of AI development are actually doing, to make the choices that compound over time toward human capability rather than dependency, and to direct the attention and resources your breakthrough has earned toward the communities and questions the industry consistently leaves out of the room.

The essay is long. Start anywhere. The argument holds from any direction.

I

Start With What They Got Right

In May 2026, Thinking Machines Lab released a research preview of what they call interaction models — AI systems designed not to wait for a human prompt but to engage in continuous, real-time exchange across audio, video, and text.[1] The model processes input and output in 200-millisecond chunks — roughly the speed of human reaction time. It can interrupt, react to silence, track elapsed time, and respond to visual cues without being explicitly told to.

To understand why this matters, it helps to be precise about what every other major AI system currently does — and does not do. GPT, Claude, Gemini, and their contemporaries all operate on a turn-based interface: you formulate a complete thought, submit it, wait one to five seconds, receive a complete response, then repeat. Even “voice mode” implementations are structurally turn-based — the model stops processing between your turns. This is not how human collaboration works. It is how you interact with a vending machine.

The most valuable applications of AI — medical consultation while a doctor examines a patient, real-time tutoring as a student works through a problem, collaborative editing while a writer thinks out loud, crisis support as someone tries to articulate distress — all require something turn-based systems structurally cannot provide: continuous, contextual presence that adjusts as the human adjusts, without requiring the human to stop, formulate, and submit. A teacher cannot pause a student’s moment of breakthrough to compose a prompt. A therapist cannot interrupt a session to type a query. Turn-based AI reaches these domains and then hits a wall that is not a technical problem but an architectural one.

Thinking Machines’ model breaks that architecture. The 200-millisecond latency, the continuous audio and video processing, the interruption capability, the time-awareness — these are not incremental improvements on existing systems. They represent a genuine change in what the human-AI relationship can look like in practice. For the first time, an AI system can track the elapsed silence between a question and an answer and understand that silence as meaningful. It can notice a shift in a person’s posture or tone without being told to look for it. It can be interrupted and redirected mid-response the way a colleague can, rather than running its full output and waiting for the next turn.

Their core critique of the current paradigm is also correct: today’s systems push humans out of the loop not by replacing them but by requiring them to translate every thought into a formal query and then step back while the machine responds. The interaction model addresses this genuinely. Thinking Machines wants AI that works with people the way people work with each other.

That is a real and important ambition. The question is whether the architecture, the framing, and the competitive market it is being released into will actually deliver it — or whether the warm aspiration will be captured by something colder.

To answer that, we have to go somewhere that AI benchmarks never go: into history.

II

How Power Builds Containers for Abuse

History does not repeat, but it does rhyme in its structures. The enclosure movement in England between the 13th and 19th centuries offers a pattern that deserves serious attention here.[2] For centuries, rural communities maintained common land — shared pastures, forests, and waterways that anyone could use to graze animals, gather food, and sustain a household. The enclosures privatized that land, piece by piece, under legal frameworks that looked perfectly reasonable in their own terms. The result was ecological disconnection, social stratification, and the funneling of human labor into wage-dependent industrial systems.

The philosopher Ivan Illich, writing in Tools for Conviviality in 1973, identified this as a template.[3] He called it the creation of a radical monopoly: when a tool or institution grows so large and so embedded that it eliminates the conditions under which alternatives could exist. The car created a radical monopoly on movement — not by forcing people into cars, but by redesigning cities so that not having a car became practically impossible. Compulsory schooling created a radical monopoly on learning — not by banning other forms of education, but by making the credential the prerequisite for everything else.

Convivial tools are those which give each person who uses them the greatest opportunity to enrich the environment with the fruits of his or her vision. Industrial tools deny this possibility. — Ivan Illich, Tools for Conviviality (1973)[3]

The question Illich forces us to ask of any technology is: does this tool extend human agency, or does it create a monopoly over a domain of human life that gradually makes unmediated human action feel inadequate, inefficient, or obsolete?

AI is in the process of answering that question right now. And the answer is not predetermined. It depends entirely on choices being made in labs, in funding rounds, in procurement decisions, and in policy rooms — most of which are happening without meaningful public deliberation.

III

Intelligence as a Word With a Body Count

Thinking Machines labels the Y-axis of their benchmark chart Intelligence. It is a small typographic choice with a long, violent history. And Thinking Machines is not alone. In May 2026, a site called AI IQ (aiiq.org) launched to widespread attention, plotting more than 50 frontier AI models on a standard human IQ bell curve — complete with a normal distribution, a right tail of high scorers, and estimated quotients reaching 136.[22] The backlash was immediate. “It’s nonsense. AI is far too jagged. The map is not the territory,” wrote one commentator. “As useless as human IQ testing,” wrote another. But the site also drew enthusiastic praise from enterprise technologists who said it made “an impossibly complex market legible.”

Notice what is happening in that praise. Enterprise technologists — the people who procure AI systems for organizations — found the IQ frame useful. Not useful for understanding what the models actually do. Useful for making purchasing decisions legible. The IQ score does not describe the system’s capabilities to the people affected by those decisions. It describes the system’s capabilities to the people making them. And those are almost never the same people.

This is the pattern that repeats across every institutionalization of a cognitive ranking framework: it is adopted first in rooms where decisions about other people’s lives are made, by people who will never be on the receiving end of its scores. The IQ test was not dangerous when Alfred Binet used it with individual children in a Paris classroom. It became dangerous when it was adopted by immigration officials, eugenicists, and courts — people with power over those being measured, operating at scale, with no feedback mechanism linking their decisions to the harm they caused. The technology of ranking moved from the specialist’s context into the institution’s logic, and the institution’s logic had nothing in it to stop the harm.

We are watching the same migration happen in real time, faster and at far greater scale. The AI IQ bell curve that enterprise buyers find useful today becomes the procurement rubric that determines which AI systems are deployed in schools, hospitals, courts, and social services. The people who will live with those deployments — the student whose essay is graded by a model ranked at IQ 118, the welfare applicant whose claim is processed by a system at IQ 112, the defendant whose risk score is generated by a model at IQ 126 — are not in the room when the purchase order is signed. They are the territory. The map is not them.

When Alfred Binet created the first intelligence test in 1904, he built a narrow tool for a narrow purpose: identifying children in French schools who needed extra support.[4] He was explicit that his scale could not and should not be used as a general ranking of human worth. Within two decades, American psychologists had stripped that nuance entirely, redeploying the tool as a measure of fixed, heritable, native intelligence.

The consequences were not abstract. Intelligence scores became the basis for immigration restriction in 1924,[5] for forced sterilization laws — upheld by the US Supreme Court — that affected over 60,000 Americans by 1964,[6] and for policies in Nazi Germany that authorized the murder of children on grounds of cognitive inferiority. The eugenicists did not think of themselves as monsters. They thought of themselves as scientists acting on data.

We are not saying Thinking Machines is engaged in eugenics thinking. We are saying: the structural move is identical. A single numerical score. A ranked comparison between systems. A label — Intelligence — that carries the full weight of human cognitive life. The intent is entirely different. The architecture is not.

Once that architecture is normalized across an industry, it does not stay in the lab. It migrates to hiring systems, to welfare eligibility decisions, to criminal sentencing tools, to educational sorting. We have already watched this happen with simpler AI systems. We are now watching it accelerate.

IV

Warm Data and What Gets Lost

Nora Bateson, President of the International Bateson Institute, offers a framework that cuts directly to why this reduction is dangerous. She calls it Warm Data.[7]

Warm Data is information that is alive. Warm Data itches when it is confined. It is the kind of information that lets you know when to tell someone you love them. — Nora Bateson[7]

Cold data — the kind that fills benchmark tables — captures a subject by removing it from context and freezing it in time. It is precise, comparable, and useful for narrow tasks. But living systems — organisms, families, communities, minds — cannot be understood from outside their relational context without losing the most important thing about them.

Bateson is not anti-data. She is explicit that warm and cold data are not opposites: they could, she says, “be friends, be in tandem to each other.” The problem is when cold data claims to have captured the whole — when a benchmark score is called Intelligence rather than what it is: a narrow measure of audio task performance in controlled conditions.

The warm data of a conversation includes everything that a real-time AI model is explicitly designed to perceive but cannot actually experience: the grief behind a question, the social context shaping how someone is holding their body, the history between two people that makes a single word carry enormous weight. Thinking Machines’ model can track time and interrupt at the right moment. It cannot know what the moment costs someone to be in.

This is not a technical limitation that will be solved by scaling. It is a categorical difference between living systems and static ones. And the danger is not that we fail to notice it — it is that in the competitive pressure to benchmark and rank and ship, we gradually stop treating it as relevant.

The risk is not that AI becomes too intelligent. The risk is that we accept its frozen capture of intelligence as sufficient — and in doing so, stop practising the living version ourselves.
V

Capital, Commodification, and the Capture of Human Expression

None of this is happening in a vacuum. It is happening inside a specific economic structure in which the development of AI is almost entirely funded by, governed by, and oriented toward the logic of capital accumulation.

In 2024, nearly 90 percent of notable AI models came from industry, up from 60 percent in 2023.[8] The research agenda — including what gets measured, what gets named, what gets optimized — is shaped by what attracts investment and competitive advantage. Thinking Machines is no exception. Their interaction model is a research preview with a commercial horizon. The benchmark chart that labels AI performance Intelligence is also, implicitly, a marketing document.

This is not a condemnation of the researchers involved, many of whom are genuinely motivated by the collaborative vision they describe. It is a structural observation: when human capabilities become the thing that AI is benchmarked against, those capabilities also become, in the logic of markets, the cost that AI is being hired to eliminate. Human judgment, human attention, human relationship — these are reframed as inefficiencies to be automated rather than values to be supported.

We can see this logic playing out in real time. An estimated 85 million jobs have been displaced globally by AI and automation through 2026.[9] Goldman Sachs models project that approximately 300 million full-time jobs worldwide will be affected by generative AI.[10] Young workers are disproportionately impacted — unemployment among 20-to-30-year-olds in tech-exposed occupations has risen by nearly 3 percentage points since early 2025 alone.[9] We are currently in what researchers describe as the period of maximum disruption: the phase when jobs hollowed out by task automation now require full transition, and the gap between displaced roles and new ones is widest.

The World Economic Forum projects that 170 million new jobs will emerge by 2030 while 92 million disappear.[11] The net is positive on paper. But the people who lose the jobs and the people who fill the new ones are rarely the same people. The critical variable — as researchers are careful to note — is the transition period: how long it takes, what support exists, and who bears the cost. The answer, historically, is: those with the least power bear the most cost and receive the least support.

The displacement effects of AI are not evenly distributed. High-wage, highly skilled roles increase while low-wage roles decrease, driving employment polarization and intensifying socioeconomic inequality — particularly in regions and industries with limited access to reskilling programs. This is not a prediction. It is the current, observed empirical record.

Meanwhile, the companies building these systems are consolidating wealth, data, and decision-making power at a pace that has no historical precedent. We are witnessing, in Illich’s terms, the construction of a radical monopoly over cognitive labor — not forced by law but created by a market logic in which AI tools gradually become so embedded in every institution that human performance without them begins to look inadequate by design.

The means of production for cognitive work — writing, analysis, design, code, synthesis, judgment — are moving rapidly into the hands of a handful of infrastructure and model owners. Workers do not own the tools; they pay subscription fees for access. This is the enclosure pattern applied to the mind. For centuries, the enclosure movement converted common land — held collectively, worked by those who depended on it — into private property that the same workers then had to rent back to survive. The AI equivalent is already underway: the collective output of human knowledge, creativity, and expression — books, code, art, conversation — was scraped without consent or compensation to train the models. Those models are now sold back to the people whose labor trained them, at monthly subscription rates, to do the work that labor used to do. The extraction is recursive: your past cognitive output trains the system that competes with your future cognitive labor, and you pay for the privilege of accessing it.

The question of who owns the tools of cognitive production is not a secondary concern. It is the central question that determines whose interests the technology serves. A hammer owned by a carpenter serves the carpenter. A hammer owned by a construction conglomerate and rented to the carpenter by the hour serves the conglomerate — and the carpenter’s dependency on it deepens with every nail driven. When the carpenter can no longer work without the rented hammer, the price goes up. And when the hammer can drive nails without the carpenter, the carpenter is released. This is not a metaphor about the future. It is a description of what is currently happening in legal research, medical imaging, financial analysis, software development, and customer service — simultaneously, in every sector, with no coordinated response.

VI

Who Is Affected That We Are Not Thinking About

There is an assumption embedded in every AI product launch, benchmark chart, and investment thesis: that the relevant actors are the labs building the systems, the enterprises deploying them, and the knowledge workers using them. The people most affected by these systems are rarely in the room when decisions are made.

Consider who is not at the Thinking Machines benchmark review:

The care worker whose job involves maintaining warm, contextual, relational presence with elderly or disabled people — and whose role is being evaluated for AI substitution on the basis of task efficiency rather than relational quality. The community organizer in a rural or postindustrial region whose civic infrastructure — local government services, social programs, community institutions — is being progressively automated in the name of cost reduction. The child growing up in a system where teachers are being replaced by AI tutors ranked by instruction-following accuracy rather than their capacity to perceive the warm data of a child’s development. The worker in the Global South whose creative and cognitive labor — art, writing, code, design — was scraped to train these models without consent or compensation, and whose market is now being supplied by the system trained on their work.

These people are not edge cases. They are the majority of the world. And the benchmarks that determine which AI systems get built, scaled, and deployed are constructed with almost no reference to their lives, their contexts, or their needs.

This is not unique to AI. It is the standard operating procedure of technology development under capital. But AI’s speed, scale, and depth of penetration into every domain of social life make the stakes qualitatively different from previous technological transitions. We do not have decades to course-correct. The systems being deployed right now are shaping what becomes infrastructurally normal within a few years.

VII

The Democratic Stakes

The risk is not only economic. It is constitutional in the deepest sense.

AI surveillance systems are already being used by authoritarian governments to monitor, target, and silence dissent. More than a third of humanity lives under autocratic rule.[12] Chinese-developed “safe city” packages are being installed from Southeast Asia to Latin America.[13] AI-based systems reduce structural checks on executive authority and concentrate power among fewer and fewer people. Once entrenched, these systems are exceedingly difficult to dismantle.

But the democratic erosion is not only happening in authoritarian states. A 2024 survey found that 64 percent of US federal government employees reported using AI applications daily or several times a week — in areas including border patrol, biometric data collection, and drone manufacturing.[14] The social media surveillance program “Catch and Revoke,” launched in March 2025 by the State Department, used AI to review tens of thousands of student visa holders’ social media.[15] Whether you find that program reasonable or horrifying, it illustrates the dynamic: AI’s promise of prediction and control creates institutional appetite for more of it, in domains that were previously governed by slower, messier, more accountable human processes.

Democracy depends on the kind of friction that AI is specifically designed to eliminate: the slowness that creates deliberation, the ambiguity that requires judgment, the relational texture that makes accountability possible. When we automate the friction, we automate away the conditions that make democratic governance function.

VIII

The Classroom as Early Warning System

If you want to know what AI is doing to human cognition, do not look at benchmark charts. Look at a first-year university writing seminar.

Educators across disciplines are reporting a convergence of symptoms they have not encountered before. Students who use AI to draft their essays are producing work that is, by most surface measures, technically correct — well-structured, grammatically clean, competently hedged. And yet something is missing that is hard to name but impossible to ignore. The work does not sound like anyone. It sounds like everyone.

Research published in Trends in Cognitive Sciences in early 2026 found that large language models are systematically homogenizing human expression across three dimensions: language, perspective, and reasoning.[16] Educators are watching this play out in real time. At Yale, a student reported that in an economics seminar that uses cold-calling, she could observe classmates pre-loading every document into a chatbot before class — not to cheat, but because the AI response felt more reliable than their own thinking.

Students face a dilemma: choosing between producing a text that “sounds better” thanks to AI, or one that “sounds like themselves.” This tension can result in writing that dilutes the student’s authentic voice — which is the essence of their identity in writing. — Frontiers in Education, 2025 synthesis of AI writing research[17]

The research on what is being lost is accumulating rapidly. Studies find that AI-supported students score better on fluency and elaboration in idea generation tasks — but also exhibit cognitive fixation and lower creative confidence, over-relying on AI suggestions as a ceiling rather than a floor. Researchers describe a pattern of cognitive offloading: the effortful work of forming a thought, finding words, tolerating the discomfort of not knowing — work that is, developmentally, precisely the point — is being bypassed. The shortcut feels like efficiency. It functions as atrophy.

For English language learners and second-language writers, the stakes are even more pointed. Writing in a second language is not merely a technical skill. It is an act of identity construction — of bringing a self, with its particular history and cultural inheritance, into relationship with a new linguistic world. Research on multilingual writers finds that without critical engagement with AI output, students risk homogenizing their writing and losing the individual markers of who they are. One student, recognising the problem, put it with remarkable clarity: “Our voice is unique, even though there are some grammar mistakes or the flow doesn’t run very smooth — but that is all better than the same structures from AI.”[17]

A parallel body of research has identified what it calls cultural ghosting — the process by which AI writing assistants, responding to benign requests like “make this more professional,” systematically remove culturally specific linguistic markers while preserving the semantic content.[18] Nigerian English, Indian English, AAVE, Singaporean English — the features that carry social meaning, that locate a writer inside a community and a history — are treated as errors to be corrected toward a standard that is, invariably, a Western professional norm. The meaning survives. The person disappears. The user did not ask to be erased. They asked for help.

What the classroom is revealing is not primarily a crisis of academic integrity. It is a crisis of formation. Writing — especially creative and personal writing — is not a way of communicating what you already think. It is a primary mechanism by which thinking happens, by which a self is built, by which a person discovers what they believe and what they value. When that process is outsourced to a system trained on the aggregate of everyone else’s expression, the loss is not just stylistic. It is ontological.

IX

Change Is Never Local: Education as Ecosystem Signal

Here is the move that systems thinking forces us to make: stop treating the classroom as a separate domain.

What is happening to writing in universities is also happening to creative work in the labour market. It is happening to the quality of political speech as elected officials use AI to generate communications at scale. It is happening to the texture of family life as parents use AI to draft letters, compose eulogies, write birthday messages. It is happening to religious communities where sermons are generated, to healthcare systems where clinical notes are auto-filled, to legal systems where arguments are templated.

Each of these looks, locally, like a reasonable efficiency gain. Collectively, they constitute a single systemic shift: the progressive replacement of human expression — in its particular, situated, culturally located form — with a statistical average of human expression trained on the past. The signal appears in education first because education is where the formation process is most explicit and most visible. But the formation that is being disrupted is not only educational. It is civic, relational, familial, spiritual, and political simultaneously.

This is exactly the pattern that Donella Meadows described in Thinking in Systems — and it carries the specific risk she identified as most dangerous: a system with an unchecked reinforcing loop that does not self-correct until it is too late, because the feedback signal — the loss of creative voice, relational texture, civic expressiveness — is diffuse, slow, and invisible against the clear short-term gains of fluency and speed.[19]

Meadows wrote that any system that “gets so encrusted that it cannot self-evolve — that systematically scorns experimentation and wipes out the raw material of variability — cuts back resilience.”[19] The raw material of variability she was describing is precisely what AI homogenization is currently trimming from human expression: not all at once, but incrementally, across every domain where the tool has been introduced, all simultaneously.

Everything mirrors everything else. The classroom mirrors the economy, which mirrors the political system, which mirrors the family, which mirrors the community’s capacity for meaning-making. These are not separate conversations. They are one conversation about what kind of cognitive and relational infrastructure a society needs to remain coherent — and what happens when that infrastructure is quietly outsourced to systems that cannot feel what they are replacing.

The raw material of variability — of distinct voices, situated knowledges, particular ways of seeing — is exactly what resilience is made of. We are trimming it at scale, in every domain, simultaneously, in exchange for fluency.
X

Systems Thinking and the Leverage We Keep Missing

Donella Meadows spent decades building a framework for understanding how to change complex systems. Her insight — distilled into what she called leverage points — was that most places where we try to intervene in systems are low-leverage: adjusting parameters, tweaking numbers, changing flows.[19] High-leverage interventions are structural and paradigmatic: changing the rules of a system, changing its goals, and — the most powerful of all — transcending the paradigm itself.

What is the paradigm out of which the current AI development ecosystem arises? It is, at its core, the belief that intelligence is a property of individual systems, that it can be measured and ranked, that more of it is unambiguously better, and that the role of technology is to produce and concentrate it. This paradigm makes the benchmark chart legible. It makes the competition between labs feel meaningful. It makes the replacement of human judgment with AI outputs feel like progress.

But the deepest leverage point — the one Meadows reserved for last — is not changing the paradigm. It is recognizing that all paradigms are constructs. That the map is not the territory. That intelligence as a rankable scalar property of individual systems is a story we are telling — and that we could, if we chose, tell a different one.

Complexity science offers that different story. Its central insight is that the most important properties of complex systems — resilience, adaptability, creativity, coherence — are not properties of individual components. They are properties of relationships between components. They are emergent. They cannot be optimized in any one element without attending to the relational ecology that produces them.

An immune system is resilient not because any single cell is maximally capable, but because the system maintains diversity of response and the capacity to recognise what it has not seen before. A forest is resilient not because any single tree is strongest, but because the mycorrhizal network redistributes resources across the whole in response to local stress. A community is resilient not because any individual is most skilled, but because the relational fabric is dense enough to absorb shock, redistribute burden, and generate new responses from the interaction of different knowledge traditions.

None of these properties survive the radical monopoly. When you optimize a forest for timber yield, you lose the network. When you optimize a classroom for measurable outcomes, you lose the relational texture of pedagogy. When you optimize human communication for fluency and correctness, you lose the particular, located, culturally distinct voice that makes each contribution irreplaceable in the system it belongs to.

The narrative shift complexity science demands is from better to more alive. Not: which system scores higher? But: which system maintains the conditions for continued self-organisation, diversity, and response? Not: how do we produce more intelligence? But: how do we tend the ecology in which many different kinds of intelligence can continue to exist and interact?

Meadows’ Highest Leverage Point — Applied to AI

The power to transcend paradigms — to recognise that no particular model of the world is absolute truth — is the most powerful place to intervene in a system.

Applied to AI development: the paradigm to transcend is not any particular benchmark or architecture. It is the belief that intelligence can be extracted from its relational context, measured, ranked, and optimised in isolation. Every design decision that reinforces that belief is low-leverage, no matter how technically sophisticated. Every design decision that challenges it — that asks instead: what are the conditions under which many different kinds of minds can flourish together? — is operating at the leverage point that actually changes systems.

XI

What Survives Collapse: The Evidence We Keep Overlooking

We are living inside multiple overlapping systemic stresses simultaneously. Climate disruption. Economic polarisation. Democratic erosion. Institutional delegitimisation. Accelerating technological change without corresponding social adaptation. Researchers have a name for this convergence — the polycrisis — and one of its defining features is that the crises are not independent.[20] They interact, amplify each other, and produce cascades that no single intervention can address.

In this context, the question of what survives states of collapse deserves to be taken seriously — not as a dystopian thought experiment but as a design constraint. What has actually survived civilizational stress, historically?

The evidence is consistent and underexamined. What survives is not the most technologically sophisticated system. It is not the highest-performing individual. What survives is relational density: the quality and depth of connections between people, and between people and their land, knowledge traditions, and shared meaning-making practices.[21]

Research on Indigenous communities facing environmental and colonial stress shows that resilience emerges from collective identity, narrative capacity, and the maintenance of local knowledge traditions that carry practical wisdom about how to live in a specific place.[21] The Byzantine Empire survived the fall of Rome not by being more powerful, but by maintaining adaptive governance and relational networks dense enough to absorb and respond to change. The Maya cities that collapsed did so when resource competition destroyed social cohesion — when the bonds enabling coordinated response disintegrated under pressure, leaving sophisticated civilisations unable to mount collective responses to crises they had the technical knowledge to address.

Across these cases, the pattern is the same. What fails first is not infrastructure or technology. What fails first is the social fabric that enables people to trust each other, communicate across difference, share resources, and generate collective responses to novel challenges. And what threatens that fabric most is precisely the dynamic we are currently accelerating: the progressive intermediation of human relationships by systems that optimise for efficiency, fluency, and scale — quietly hollowing out the practice of genuine contact with another person.

What survives collapse is the capacity for bricolage — for improvising, combining available resources in ways not tried before. That capacity lives in diverse, locally-rooted, relationally-connected communities with deep knowledge of their particular contexts. It does not live in systems trained on the statistical aggregate of everything. The aggregate of everything is, by definition, the average — and the average is exactly what fails when conditions move outside the range the training data has seen.

Indigenous resilience research consistently identifies narrative capacity — the ability to make meaning from catastrophe, to locate suffering in a larger story, to imagine and communicate a future — as among the most critical survival resources. This is not a soft cultural finding. It is an empirical observation about what keeps communities coherent enough to act collectively under extreme stress. It is warm data. And we are training the next generation to outsource it.
XII

Alternative Frameworks: Seeing the Longer Arc

None of this is inevitable. But avoiding these outcomes requires frameworks that can hold more complexity than a benchmark chart allows. Here are some that offer genuine traction.

Warm Data — Nora Bateson

Core Principle

Prioritize transcontextual, relational information over decontextualized metrics. Before asking “how do we measure this?” ask “what are the interrelationships that give this system its vitality — and which of them will a measurement approach destroy?”

Applied to AI development: before deploying any system into a social domain, map the warm data of that domain. What relational qualities currently make it function? Which of those will survive automation, and which will be quietly eliminated in the efficiency gain?

Convivial Tools — Ivan Illich

Core Principle

A tool is convivial when it gives each person who uses it the greatest opportunity to enrich their world with the fruits of their own vision. It becomes anti-convivial when it creates a radical monopoly — when it grows so dominant that not using it becomes a form of exclusion.

Applied to AI development: at what point does an AI system stop extending human capability and start replacing the conditions under which unaugmented human capability is treated as sufficient? Build for that threshold explicitly, rather than discovering it after the fact.

Symmathesy — Mutual Learning Systems

Core Principle

Also from Bateson: symmathesy is the process by which living systems — individuals, families, communities — learn together through mutual responsiveness over time.[7] It is not one system optimizing another. It is a living ecology of co-adaptation.

Applied to AI development: the design goal should not be an AI that is better at tasks than humans, but an AI-human ecology in which both become more capable together. This is a fundamentally different optimization target than benchmark performance.

Participatory Justice — Community-Oriented Design

Core Principle

Decisions that affect communities should be made with those communities, not for them. The people most exposed to a technology’s risks should have meaningful power over the terms of its deployment.

Applied to AI development: any lab serious about pro-social AI should build formal mechanisms for community deliberation into its development process — not as a compliance checkbox but as a genuine source of design requirements.

XIII

Who Wins, Who Loses, and How the Winners Act

The distribution of benefit from AI is not opaque. It follows a pattern visible in every prior technology transition, and the names of the winners are already known.

The infrastructure layer is controlled by three companies — Amazon, Google, and Microsoft — whose cloud services provide the compute on which nearly all frontier AI models run. NVIDIA controls approximately 80 percent of the GPU market used for AI training and inference. These companies do not need to win the model race. They win regardless of which model wins, because they own the ground on which all models are built. The race between labs is, from this vantage point, a marketing contest that drives demand for infrastructure the race-holders do not own.

The model layer is controlled by a small number of labs — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta AI — whose capital requirements for frontier model training have now reached billions of dollars per training run. That capital threshold is not accidental. It functions as a moat. A researcher who could approach frontier capability independently in 2020 cannot do so in 2026 without access to infrastructure owned by the same companies they would be competing with. The barrier to entry is not technical genius. It is access to compute owned by incumbents.

The capital layer sits above both. SoftBank, Microsoft, Google, Andreessen Horowitz, Tiger Global, and a small number of sovereign wealth funds have placed bets across the entire AI stack simultaneously — infrastructure, models, applications, and the data pipelines feeding all three. Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, has publicly sought to raise $7 trillion for AI infrastructure — a figure exceeding the GDP of Japan — while OpenAI transitions from the nonprofit structure that justified its founding into a fully for-profit corporation. The philanthropic frame that created public goodwill and regulatory deference is being dissolved precisely as the commercial opportunity it deferred to has clarified.

The regulatory layer is being shaped in real time, and the incumbents are shaping it. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google have all participated extensively in AI safety and governance discussions in Washington, Brussels, and London. The regulations that emerge tend to impose compliance costs — auditing requirements, safety evaluations, documentation standards — that well-resourced incumbents can absorb and that smaller competitors, open-source projects, and non-Western labs cannot. This is regulatory capture disguised as responsibility. The safety frame is not false — frontier AI does carry real risks — but the regulatory framework built around that frame consistently advantages the labs that wrote it.

The losers are more numerous and less organized. Shoshana Zuboff’s research on surveillance capitalism named the dynamic a decade before it reached its current scale: the raw material of human experience — attention, behavior, expression, relationship — is extracted at no cost, rendered into data, and sold as predictions about future behavior.[23] With generative AI, the extraction deepens. The raw material now includes the full output of human creative and cognitive labor: every book, article, image, piece of code, and conversation that could be scraped. The people who produced that output receive nothing. The models trained on it generate billions in revenue. Kate Crawford’s Atlas of AI documents the material and human costs invisible in this equation — the cobalt mines, the content moderators, the annotators paid pennies per label to make the models safe enough to deploy.[24]

Virginia Eubanks, in Automating Inequality, traced what happens when AI systems meet the people who had the least power before they arrived: the welfare applicant whose benefits are cut by an algorithm that processes her file without ever meeting her; the unhoused person whose housing priority is scored by a system trained on data that encodes the biases of the system that failed her.[25] Ruha Benjamin, in Race After Technology, named the same dynamic in policing, healthcare, and hiring: algorithms do not eliminate discrimination; they launder it — convert human bias into mathematical output that carries the authority of objectivity while remaining perfectly legible to the structures of power that produced the bias in the first place.[26]

How the winners act — once the position is secured — also follows a pattern. First, they embed before the rules are set. Once AI is integrated into hospital records systems, government benefits processing, educational platforms, and financial infrastructure, the switching cost becomes prohibitive. The integration becomes the monopoly. Second, they frame incumbency as safety: only large, well-resourced labs can be trusted to build and govern powerful AI responsibly. This is sometimes true and always convenient. Third, they convert dependency into subscription: tools that began as productivity aids become infrastructure that institutions cannot function without, and pricing adjusts accordingly. The carpenter who cannot work without the rented hammer does not negotiate from strength.

The cotton gin offers the clearest historical warning. Eli Whitney invented it believing it would reduce the need for enslaved labor — cotton processing was the bottleneck; remove the bottleneck and the system becomes less profitable. Instead, it massively increased the profitability of cotton cultivation, expanded the plantation system, and intensified slavery. The inventor’s intent was irrelevant to the structural outcome. What mattered was what the tool made profitable for those who controlled the land. The lesson for AI: the question is never only what the technology can do. It is what becomes profitable to do with it, for whom, and who controls the conditions under which it is deployed.
XIV

The Way Out: From Solidarity to Community Ownership

None of this is inevitable. But the alternatives require deliberate construction, not spontaneous emergence. Here are paths that exist — not as proposals but as operating realities.

Worker Data Organizing and Data Unions

The Model

Workers collectively bargain not only over wages and conditions but over how their labor output — their writing, code, creative work, behavioral data — is used by AI systems. Data unions negotiate licensing terms on behalf of members, require consent for training data use, and channel revenue from AI-generated work back to the workers whose output trained the model.

Several journalism unions have begun negotiating AI licensing terms with publishers. The Screen Actors Guild and Writers Guild of America have both secured contract provisions governing the use of members’ performances and scripts in AI training. These are early and partial victories, but they establish the legal and organizational precedent that worker data is negotiable — not simply extractable.

Open-Source Model Commons

The Model

Platforms like Hugging Face maintain a commons of open model weights, datasets, and tools that anyone can access, fine-tune, and build on without API fees or proprietary lock-in. Models like Mistral, LLaMA, and Falcon operate at near-frontier capability at a fraction of the cost of closed models — and the communities that develop them are not answerable to the same investment logic as closed labs.

The GPL (General Public License) — Richard Stallman’s “copyleft” framework — established the legal principle that free software must remain free: anyone who distributes a modified version must release their modifications under the same terms. Applied to AI, this principle would require that models built on open training data and open weights cannot be enclosed by a downstream commercial actor. The legal frameworks for this are nascent but developing.

Platform Cooperativism and Worker Ownership

The Model

The Mondragon Cooperative Corporation, founded in the Basque region of Spain in 1956 by a Catholic priest and five engineers, now comprises more than 80,000 worker-owners across manufacturing, retail, finance, and education. Worker-owners elect their managers, share profits, and cannot be laid off without community consent. During the 2008 financial crisis, Mondragon retrained and redeployed workers across the network rather than cutting them — a resilience that surrounding market-capitalist firms could not match.

The Driver’s Cooperative in New York City operates a ride-share platform owned by its drivers. Stocksy is a photographer-owned stock image platform. Up&Go is a worker-owned cleaning service. These are not utopian experiments. They are operating businesses that demonstrate a simple principle: when the people who do the work own the tools, the tools serve the workers. When the tools are owned by investors, the tools optimize for investor returns — and the workers are the cost to be minimized.

Applied to AI: community-owned model cooperatives, shared compute infrastructure, and worker-controlled deployment platforms are not technically impossible. They are organizationally and politically difficult in an environment where the capital concentration of incumbents is treated as inevitable. The question is whether the political will exists to treat it otherwise.

Indigenous Data Sovereignty and Community Data Trusts

The Model

The First Nations Information Governance Centre and allied bodies have articulated the principle of Indigenous data sovereignty: data about Indigenous communities belongs to those communities, is governed by their own protocols, and cannot be used without their free, prior, and informed consent. This is not a request for special treatment. It is an assertion of the same property right that corporations exercise over their proprietary data — applied to the communities whose knowledge, land, and cultural production have historically been extracted without consent.

Data trusts extend this principle more broadly: a legal structure in which data is held by an independent trustee for the benefit of the people who generated it, rather than owned outright by the platform that collected it. Estonia’s data embassies and the emerging European data governance frameworks offer partial models. The principle — that the people who generate data should have rights over how it is used — is the same principle that governs every other form of property. Its absence from AI development is a political choice, not a technical necessity.

Public AI Infrastructure

The Model

Municipal broadband networks — publicly owned internet infrastructure — have demonstrated that public ownership of critical digital infrastructure is both feasible and beneficial to communities that private providers systematically underserve. The same logic applies to AI compute: publicly funded GPU clusters, made available to researchers, civil society organizations, educators, and small businesses at cost, would break the capital moat that currently limits frontier AI development to a handful of labs.

Economist Mariana Mazzucato has documented extensively that the foundational technologies underlying AI — the internet, GPS, touchscreens, Siri, the algorithms behind Google search — were developed with substantial public investment, then privatized at the point of commercial viability.[27] The public bore the risk. The private sector captured the return. Public AI infrastructure would rebalance that equation: public investment retains public benefit, and the resulting tools are governed as commons rather than enclosures.

XV

The Next Chapter of the Play

In Greek tragedy, the chorus exists to speak what the actors cannot see. Each actor performs their role correctly. The catastrophe does not arrive because anyone makes an obvious mistake. It arrives because the structure was written so that every individually rational action combines into collective disaster. Oedipus does nothing wrong by any local measure. The tragedy is in the shape of the whole.

We are inside such a play right now. The chorus speaks to the audience. Here is what the next chapter looks like from outside it.

If you are an AI engineer building interaction models, optimization pipelines, or deployment systems: your technical choices — what to optimize, what safeguards to build in, how to document capabilities and limitations, what data to use and not use — are among the highest-stakes design decisions being made in this period of human history. The thing you are building may be genuinely useful. The structure you are building it for will concentrate the value you create in the hands of very few people, while distributing the cost across very many people who have no voice in your design decisions. Treating your work as purely technical is not a neutral position. It is a position with consequences that will outlast your employment at any particular lab.

If you are an enterprise buyer deploying AI into healthcare, education, legal services, or social programs: you are not purchasing a tool. You are making a governance decision about who has authority over judgments that previously required human accountability. The IQ score or benchmark ranking that made the purchase decision legible does not transfer accountability for the outcomes. The model cannot be sued, fired, or held responsible. You can. The people whose lives are shaped by the deployment had no voice in it, and the feedback loop from their experience to your procurement decision is, in most organizations, nonexistent. Build it deliberately or it will not exist.

If you are an investor: the returns you are seeking require labor displacement at scale, cognitive infrastructure concentration in fewer hands, and the progressive dependency of institutions on systems you own. You may believe this is net positive. The historical record of technology transitions — railways, electricity, telecommunications, early internet — suggests the net is positive on paper and distributed with extreme unevenness in practice, with the people least able to absorb disruption absorbing the most of it, and the transition period — the gap between the old system and the new — lasting a generation for those inside it. You have more capacity to reshape the terms of that distribution than almost anyone currently alive. The current deployment of that capacity is not pointed at distribution.

If you are a policymaker: the companies you are regulating are funding the research that defines what you understand about what needs to be regulated. The frameworks you are writing will govern a technology that evolves faster than any regulatory cycle. The communities most exposed to AI’s harms — low-wage workers, communities facing systemic discrimination, people in the Global South, people dependent on public services — are not in the rooms where those frameworks are written. Regulatory capture in AI governance is not a risk on the horizon. It is the current condition of the field. The question is whether you can name it clearly enough to act against it.

If you are a worker adopting AI tools to be more productive, to compete, to keep your job: the efficiency you are gaining is real. The dynamic you are participating in is also real. Each time you use an AI tool to do your job faster, you are demonstrating to your employer that the job can be done with less human labor, training the system that will eventually do it without you, and generating revenue for the infrastructure owners who have no obligation to share it with you. This is not a reason to refuse the tools. It is a reason to organize around the terms on which those tools enter your workplace — before the terms are set without you.

If you are an educator adopting AI in your classroom or institution: what you normalize becomes the baseline. The students who learn to reach for AI before reaching into themselves are learning a dependency that will define their cognitive lives. The students who learn to use AI as a scaffold — temporary support for a capability they are building — are learning something different. The difference is not in the tool. It is in the pedagogy, the culture, and the institutional values you choose to protect or surrender. Those choices are being made right now, under time pressure, without adequate support, with commercial vendors defining the terms. The next chapter of this is visible: the students who cannot write without assistance become the workers who cannot think without it, become the citizens who cannot deliberate without it. The formation happens in your classroom or it doesn’t happen.

This is not a call for Thinking Machines to stop building. Their interaction model addresses a real problem in how humans and AI systems currently relate. Real-time, collaborative, multimodal AI engagement genuinely has the potential to keep humans more meaningfully in the loop — if the conditions supporting that are cultivated rather than eroded.

But the lab has stated publicly that it wants feedback. Here, then, is substantive feedback — not about the model’s latency scores, but about the world it is being built into and what it might cultivate or foreclose:

Rename the benchmark axis. Call it what it is: audio reasoning task performance, instruction-following accuracy, or response quality. Intelligence is a word with a body count. Precision is not pedantry here — it is a refusal to feed a framework with a dangerous history.

Map the warm data of your target domains. Before deploying interaction models in healthcare, education, social services, or governance, conduct genuine inquiry into what relational qualities currently make those domains function. Commission that work from sociologists, community organizers, and the people most affected — not just from engineers and product managers.

Build against the radical monopoly. Design for reversibility. Design for human capability maintenance alongside AI augmentation. Ask explicitly: what happens to the humans in this system if the AI is removed? If the answer is “they can no longer function,” the system has already created an Illichian monopoly, and that is a design failure, not a success.

Make the power distribution visible. Who profits from the systems you build? Who bears the cost of displacement? Who was not in the room when design decisions were made? Publish that accounting alongside your benchmark results.

Fund the counter-infrastructure. Thinking Machines has announced a research grant for interactivity benchmarks. Direct a meaningful portion of that funding toward the social science of AI impacts — specifically to communities and researchers outside the existing AI ecosystem who can surface what the ecosystem cannot see about itself.

XVI

What Kind of World Are We Cultivating?

Every technology embeds a vision of the future in its design choices. The interaction model embeds a vision in which humans and AI are genuinely collaborative partners — in which the AI adapts to human rhythm, human context, human need, rather than requiring humans to flatten themselves into prompts and wait. That is a better vision than most of what the industry is currently building toward. And the problem Thinking Machines solved — the turn-based bottleneck — is a real one. Continuous, low-latency, multimodal AI opens domains that were previously architecturally closed: real-time medical decision support, genuine tutoring presence, collaborative creative work that doesn’t interrupt its own flow. These are not trivial.

But every significant problem solved also unlocks new vectors of harm proportional to the significance of the solution. A system that can engage continuously, read affect, track elapsed time, and respond to visual cues without being prompted is not only a better collaborator. It is a more capable surveillance instrument. A more persuasive sales presence. A more effective tool for keeping a vulnerable person engaged with a platform past the point of their own wellbeing. A more plausible impersonator of a therapist, a teacher, a friend. The same continuity that makes it useful for care makes it dangerous in the hands of actors who benefit from dependency, compliance, and the erosion of human judgment. The architecture does not distinguish between those use cases. The market will not either, without deliberate intervention.

The bad actors are not hypothetical. Authoritarian governments that already deploy AI for surveillance will integrate continuous interaction models into monitoring systems that never stop listening. Predatory lenders and gambling platforms that already use behavioral nudging will use affect-aware AI to identify and exploit the precise moment of cognitive vulnerability. Social media platforms that already optimize for engagement over wellbeing will use real-time emotional tracking to hold attention at the cost of the person holding it. Political operations that already deploy disinformation at scale will use interaction models to make that disinformation personally responsive — adaptive, contextual, and impossible to dismiss as obviously automated.

None of these uses require the technology to be misappropriated. They require only that it be deployed for the purposes its funders have always funded it for: profit, control, and the consolidation of advantage. A good tool released into a structure without deliberate countermeasures will be used to serve that structure’s logic. That is not pessimism. That is the record.

Bateson writes that regeneration is participating in life that makes more life.[7] A meadow does this. A family does this. A community does this. The question for every AI system being built right now is the same question: does this participate in life that makes more life, or does it extract and optimize the residue of life after the warm data has been stripped out?

The Mondragon cooperatives asked this question in 1956 in a Basque village with five worker-owners and a priest. They are still asking it, with 80,000 worker-owners, seven decades later. The GPL asked this question in 1989, in a software ecosystem already being enclosed by proprietary vendors. The legal commons it built still underlies most of the internet. Indigenous data sovereignty movements are asking this question right now, in the face of systems trained on their knowledge without consent, asserting that the principle of community ownership over collectively generated knowledge is not archaic — it is the only durable alternative to extraction.

The question does not need to be answered completely to be held seriously. It needs to be held as the primary design constraint — not a secondary consideration after the benchmarks are satisfied, the investment closed, the product shipped.

The hammer is extraordinary. The problem is not whether to use it. The problem is that the people who own the hammer are not the people who will live in what it builds. They will be long paid and long gone when the structure settles. The people who will live in it — the workers who were displaced during the transition, the students whose formation was shaped by what their teachers had time for, the communities whose institutions were automated before anyone asked whether they should be — are the ones for whom the question of what kind of world we are building is not rhetorical.

They are the ones who need us to stop performing our roles and read the whole play.
